Visible-Light Activation of Alkyl Chlorides

This project focused on the development of visible-light photoredox methodologies for the activation of unactivated alkyl chlorides, enabling radical cyclization reactions under mild conditions with first-row transition metal catalysts.

Key Scientific Advances
C(sp³)–Cl activation

Visible-light activation of challenging alkyl chloride substrates.

Mechanistic insight

Combination of experimental studies and DFT-calculations to understand the reaction pathway.

Technology transfer

The work contributed to a European patent on photocatalytic activation of organic chlorides.

Experimental set-up
Key mechanistic discoveries

Comprehensive mechanistic investigations combining spectroscopy, electrochemistry, EPR, NMR, synchrotron studies and DFT calculations were carried out to elucidate the activation pathway of unactivated alkyl chlorides and identify the key reactive intermediates involved in the catalytic cycle.

Mechanistic Studies.
Proposed Mechanism.
Mechanistic interpretation

Mechanistic investigations revealed the formation of highly nucleophilic low-valent Ni(I) intermediates capable of activating unactivated alkyl chlorides through an SN2-type oxidative addition pathway. Subsequent generation of carbon-centered radicals enables the cyclization step, while spectroscopic, electrochemical and computational studies provided a coherent picture of the overall catalytic cycle.

Related outputs

[2] Angew. Chem. Int. Ed. 2019, [3] Synlett 2019, [P1] European Patent, [1] ISHC 2018.

Decatungstate Ionic Liquid Photocatalyst

Development of a recyclable decatungstate-based ionic liquid photocatalyst combining the reactivity of homogeneous photocatalysis with the recoverability of supported catalytic systems. The catalyst was successfully applied to a broad range of hydrogen atom transfer transformations and recycled over multiple catalytic cycles.

Key scientific advances
Recyclable photocatalyst

Design of a recoverable decatungstate ionic liquid maintaining the reactivity of conventional TBADT systems.

Broad applicability

Application to Giese, Minisci, hydroacylation, oxidation and dual catalytic transformations.

Sustainable recovery

Catalyst recycling through simple extraction protocols with minimal activity loss.

Experimental setup
Catalyst design & characterisation

DT-IL was prepared by pairing the decatungstate anion with a long-chain organo-phosphonium cation, generating a recyclable catalytic system with improved handling properties and broad solvent compatibility.

Catalytic applications

DT-IL was applied to a diverse set of hydrogen atom transfer transformations, demonstrating broad synthetic versatility under photocatalytic conditions across different reaction classes and solvent systems.

Solvent compatibility

The ionic liquid photocatalyst displays enhanced solvent compatibility compared to TBADT, enabling efficient photocatalysis under a wider range of reaction conditions.

Catalyst recycling

DT-IL can be recovered through simple liquid-liquid extraction and reused over multiple catalytic cycles with negligible loss of activity.

Sustainability impact

The catalyst was recovered through liquid–liquid extraction and reused over five consecutive cycles with negligible loss of activity, demonstrating the feasibility of homogeneous photocatalyst recycling.

Automated CO₂ Photoreduction Coupled to Carbonylation Chemistry

This project focused on the development of an automated and modular reactor platform integrating high-temperature gas-phase photothermal CO₂ reduction to CO over coated heterogeneous catalyst beads with on-demand palladium-catalyzed carbonylative cross-coupling reactions in fed-batch mode.

Key scientific advances
Automated gas-phase reactor

Modular photoreactor platform for high-temperature CO₂ reduction under controlled light, pressure, temperature and residence time.

On-demand CO generation

Selective generation of CO from CO₂ reduction and direct integration with downstream palladium-catalyzed carbonylation chemistry.

Fed-batch carbonylation

Use of generated CO in palladium-catalyzed carbonylative cross-coupling reactions for fine-chemical synthesis.

Automated CO₂ photoreduction platform

The platform combines controlled gas delivery, high-temperature irradiation, automated pressure and flow regulation, and online analysis to evaluate heterogeneous catalysts for light-assisted CO₂ conversion.

On-demand carbonylation chemistry

The generated CO was directly used as a reagent for palladium-catalyzed carbonylative cross-couplings, linking CO₂ conversion with downstream fine-chemical synthesis.

CO2 Reduction Catalyst

The CO₂-to-CO module relies on coated glass beads as heterogeneous catalyst supports, enabling high-temperature gas-phase photoreduction under controlled irradiation and flow conditions.

Photo2Fuel

Photo2Fuel is a Horizon Europe project focused on the development of bio-hybrid systems for solar-driven CO₂ conversion into fuels and chemicals. My work focused on WP4 scale-up, including the implementation of photochemical reactor platforms, light-assisted CO₂ reduction studies, and integration of polymer-dot/bacteria hybrid systems.

Project impact

This work contributed to the development of scalable reactor concepts for artificial photosynthesis, bridging photochemistry, flow reactor engineering and hybrid biological systems for CO₂ valorisation.

Key scientific advances
Hybrid solar fuels

Development of hybrid photochemical systems for light-driven CO₂ conversion.

Reactor scale-up

Implementation of LSC-PM and flow reactor platforms for controlled irradiation and scale-up studies.

WP4 leadership

Coordination of scale-up activities connecting reactor engineering, photochemistry and biocatalytic CO₂ conversion.

Photochemical reactor platform

The reactor platform was designed to enable controlled irradiation, liquid flow operation and integration with hybrid catalytic systems for solar-driven CO₂ reduction studies.

Lab-Scale Experiments

Low-scale experiments were carried out in a custom-designed 1 mL blue-light Luminescent Solar Concentrator (LSC) reactor integrated with a 3D-printed peristaltic pump and high-intensity white LEDs, forming a fully computer-controlled and automated experimental platform.

High-Scale Experiments

Scale-up studies were performed in an 18 mL transparent PMMA photoreactor fitted with FEP tubing. Reactor temperature was maintained using a PID-controlled thermal panel, while light intensity and irradiation profiles were regulated through a custom Arduino-based control system. The heterogeneous bacteria–liquid–gas suspension was continuously circulated from a thermostated reservoir through the photoreactor by means of a peristaltic pump, providing efficient mixing and consistent irradiation conditions.

Mentoring Approach

I strongly believe that people learn more effectively, become more creative, and ultimately achieve better results when they enjoy their work and feel supported throughout the process.

Curriculum Vitae

Professional Summary

My scientific career has focused on the development of sustainable chemical methodologies at the interface of catalysis, photochemistry, flow chemistry, and laboratory automation.

I began my research training at the University of Oviedo, where I completed my Bachelor’s and Master’s research projects under the supervision of Prof. Julio Pérez and Prof. Pilar Gamasa, respectively. These early experiences introduced me to ionic liquids, asymmetric catalysis, and reaction design, establishing the foundations of my interest in sustainable chemical synthesis.

I subsequently joined the Institute of Chemical Research of Catalonia (ICIQ) to pursue a PhD under the supervision of Prof. Julio Lloret-Fillol and Prof. Alicia Casitas, graduating cum laude in June 2020. My doctoral research focused on the development of visible-light photoredox methodologies for carbon–halogen bond activation, combining synthetic methodology development with mechanistic investigations and interdisciplinary collaborations.

Following my doctoral studies, I joined the University of Valladolid as a postdoctoral researcher in the organometallic chemistry group led by Prof. Ana C. Albéniz, Prof. Jesús A. Miguel, and Prof. Camino Bartolomé. There, I established photocatalysis as a new research line within the group while leading projects in sustainable organometallic catalysis and functional porous materials. This period also marked an important step in my development as a mentor and supervisor of undergraduate and doctoral researchers.

In 2023, I joined the group of Prof. Timothy Noël at the University of Amsterdam to expand my expertise in flow chemistry, reactor engineering, and automated experimentation. During my postdoctoral stay, I contributed to projects spanning artificial photosynthesis, CO₂ valorization, reactor development, and laboratory automation. My work combined chemistry, engineering, and data-driven experimentation within international collaborations focused on sustainable technologies and autonomous laboratories. During this period, I also mentored undergraduate, Master’s, and PhD students while contributing to the scientific development of several multidisciplinary research projects.

Throughout these stages, I have progressively combined expertise from different disciplines and research environments, developing an interdisciplinary approach that bridges chemistry, engineering, and automation.
